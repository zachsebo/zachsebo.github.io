As an undergrad, I worked in laboratories at the Stowers Institute, University of Missouri-Kansas City, and Mayo Clinic. My projects involved cell culture models, embryonic development, insulin signaling, and gene editing tools. I published a first-author paper describing a germline-specific CRISPR/Cas9 system in Drosophila that I developed. I received the Victoria Finnerty Award from the Genetics Society of America to present this work at the Annual Drosophila Research Conference. My senior year I was awarded a graduate research fellowship from the National Science Foundation and proceeded to carry out doctoral studies at Yale.

For my PhD thesis, I worked with Matthew Rodeheffer and published a number of articles on adipocyte differentiation and the regulation of fat distribution by sex hormones. Among other discoveries, I found that visceral fat depots are derived from distinct mesodermal subcompartments in male and female mice and that androgens block obesogenic adipogenesis in vivo. In recognition of this work, I received the Spangler Award for Outstanding PhD Thesis at Yale University.

My postdoctoral studies have been supported at various points by the National Cancer Institute, National Heart, Lung and Blood Institute, and the American Federation for Aging Research. In addition to research, I have mentored several high school and undergraduate students through the UChicago ResearcHStart, Northwestern CURE, and Kimberly Querrey summer internship programs.

Technical experience includes a range of methods in molecular biology, along with confocal microscopy, flow cytometry, RNAseq, and metabolomic analysis. Lately, I have been exploring use cases for AI tools in the computational analysis of large public data sets.
